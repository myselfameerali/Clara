on silent love, unworthiness, and the weight of choosing

A final letter · 7th April 2026
Prologue

Before I say anything, I want you to know why I'm saying it.

This isn't a confession meant to make things complicated. This isn't me asking for anything in return, or hoping to change what is. This is just me finally choosing honesty because I've spent too long choosing silence, and silence, I've learned, has its own kind of weight.

I've written this more times than I can count. In my head, at 2am, mid-conversation with myself, during quiet moments at the gym when I'd see you and suddenly forget what I was doing. I've rehearsed versions of this that were dramatic, versions that were detached, versions I deleted before finishing the first line.

This is the version that's just true.

I'm not writing this to get a reaction. I'm writing this because some things deserve to be said not because they'll change anything, but because carrying them quietly for so long starts to feel dishonest. And whatever this is, whatever I feel, it has always been honest.

So here it is. All of it.

Before Everything

I just want to make you happy. I think you are amazing, Clara.

That's it. That's the whole thing, really.

Everything else in this letter every metaphor, every carefully constructed sentence, every late-night thought I tried to shape into something worthy of you all of it is just a longer way of saying this. Two sentences. Eleven words. The simplest, most honest thing I have ever felt about another person.

I just want to make you happy.

Not in the way people say it when they want something in return. Not as a strategy, not as a way in, not as the opening line of something more complicated. Just genuinely, cleanly, without agenda, I want your life to be full of the things that make you feel good. I want your days to be lighter than your hardest ones. I want whoever is in your world to treat you the way you deserve to be treated. I want you to laugh easily and rest well and feel, at the end of each day, that the world was mostly kind to you.

That's all. That has always been all.

And I think you are amazing, Clara. Not in the way people throw that word around until it means nothing. In the specific, considered, I-have-thought-about-this-carefully way. The way that comes from actually paying attention.

You are amazing in the way that doesn't require a stage. In the quiet, everyday, completely unperformed way that is so much rarer than the loud kind. Amazing in the way you carry yourself without making it anyone else's burden. Amazing in the way you exist fully, genuinely, without apology in a world that constantly asks people to be less than they are.

I noticed. Maybe nobody told you enough. Maybe the people closest to you said it so often it stopped landing. Or maybe they didn't say it enough at all. Either way I noticed, from wherever I was standing, and I meant it every single time it crossed my mind.

You are amazing.

And I just wanted you to be happy.

Everything else I wrote every word of it begins and ends right here.

I — The Quiet Beginning

You didn't arrive like a storm.

Maybe I carry a curse the quiet kind that doesn't shatter things all at once, but slowly takes away whatever I begin to love. I've thought about this more than I'd like to admit. The people I've cared about most have always been the ones I've never fully said it to. And yet the caring never stopped. It just lived somewhere quiet inside me, like a room I kept returning to without realizing.

With you, it wasn't different. If anything, it was more of the same except this time, I noticed it earlier. This time, I watched it happen and still couldn't stop it.

You didn't arrive like a storm. You didn't announce yourself. There was no single moment where I thought, "this person is going to matter." You just… appeared. Calm and consistent, the way certain things become part of your routine without you deciding they should. A song that starts playing in the background and slowly becomes the only one you hear.

Somewhere between the ordinary moments the passing conversations, the glances across a room, the way you carried yourself through a space I started noticing you in a way that felt different. Not loud. Not desperate. Just real. The kind of noticing that happens before you've named it, before you've decided what to do with it.

And I think that's the thing about quiet feelings they don't give you a warning. They just settle in, and by the time you realize they're there, they've already made themselves at home.

I started caring not loudly, not in ways the world could see, but in quiet ways that matter more.

I remembered the little things. The way your energy shifted on certain days. The specific kind of quiet you carried when something was weighing on you. The warmth that came through when you were comfortable. These weren't things I was trying to memorize they just stayed, because when you care about someone, their details don't feel like details. They feel like the whole picture.

And maybe that's where it all began to get complicated because I don't know how to feel halfway. I never have. When I care about someone, I care fully, completely, without reservation. It's not something I decide. It's just how I'm built. And sometimes I wonder if that's a flaw, or just a different way of loving one that the world doesn't always have room for.

But I want you to know: whatever I felt, it was never a burden I placed on you. It was never your responsibility. It was just mine, mine to carry, mine to understand, mine to eventually put into words. These words.

II — Wanting vs. Keeping

There's a difference I learned because of you.

Not from a book. Not from advice someone gave me. From watching. From paying attention. From being someone who was quietly present in your world without ever quite being part of it.

The difference between someone who wants you and someone who chooses to keep you.

Wanting is easy. It requires nothing. It lives in moments in the butterflies of a first meeting, in the excitement of newness, in the thrill of something that hasn't yet become familiar. Wanting doesn't ask you to show up on hard days. It doesn't require consistency. It exists in the spaces between effort and actually doing something.

Keeping someone is different. It's unglamorous and patient and deliberate. It means choosing someone not when it's easy, but when it costs you something.

With you, everything felt calm. That was the thing I kept coming back to. There was no performance, no drama, no need to be anything other than what I was when you were around. Your presence had a way of making ordinary moments feel like enough like the world didn't need to be anything more complicated than what it already was.

I didn't need grand gestures around you. I just needed the small things a moment where the conversation flowed without effort, the kind of silence that didn't feel uncomfortable, a shared space that somehow felt lighter because you were in it. That kind of ease is rarer than people realize. Most people spend their whole lives searching for it and confusing it with boredom when they find it.

I never confused it for boredom. I knew what it was.

And then, one day, the space changed. You weren't there in the same way. The gym felt the same the same sounds, the same light, the same routine and yet something was missing in a way I couldn't explain to anyone without sounding like I was overreacting. How do you tell someone that a place feels empty not because anything changed, but because a specific person's presence made it feel full?

Silence has a way of answering questions we're too afraid to ask out loud. I didn't ask. But I heard the answer anyway.

III — Choosing

Hamesha. What that word actually means.

"Main hamesha tumhen hi chunoongi…"

I've thought about this line a lot. About what it means to say it really say it and mean every syllable of it.

It sounds simple when you read it. Soft, almost. But underneath it is something that most people never do: a choice made not once, but repeatedly, every single day, even on the days when it's hard.

Choosing someone isn't a moment. That's the thing no one tells you. It's not the grand declaration or the perfect night or the first time you realize you feel something. Those are easy. Those are the parts that feel like falling. But love real love isn't falling. It's standing. It's choosing to stay when you don't have to. It's showing up when showing up is inconvenient, or scary, or uncertain.

"Hamesha" isn't a fairytale promise. It's not forever in the way movies make you believe forever works. It's effort, repeated. It's waking up and deciding that this person with all their flaws, all their contradictions, all the parts of them that aren't easy is still the person you want to choose today.

"Tumhen hi chunoongi" isn't blindness. It's awareness clear, full awareness and still choosing. That is the bravest thing a person can do.

I think about what it means to be chosen like that. Not as a convenience, not as a default, not because nothing better came along but deliberately. Intentionally. The way you'd choose something you know has real value, not because it's perfect, but because it's real.

Real love doesn't happen in the big moments. It happens in the small, invisible, ordinary ones in the way someone remembers what you said three weeks ago, in the way they check on you when your energy is off, in the way they make space for you without being asked. In the way they stay, quietly, even when leaving would be easier.

I chose you in all the quiet ways. Maybe you never saw it. But it was there, in everything I did and didn't say.

IV — Recognition

You are rare. I need you to know that.

I don't say this lightly, and I don't say it as a compliment you're supposed to brush off. I say it because I genuinely believe it, and because I think there's a version of you that doesn't fully see it that accepts "fine" and "good enough" when you deserve so much more than that.

There are people in this world who are kind in the obvious ways polite, considerate, surface-level warm. And then there are people who are kind in the deeper sense. People who are genuine without performance. People whose presence makes you feel like you can exist without explanation. People who don't require you to shrink or expand or adjust yourself they just let you be.

You are the second kind of person. And the second kind is rare.

I've met people who were interesting. I've met people who were beautiful. I've met people who were funny and sharp and worth knowing. But the combination the way you carried yourself, the ease you brought to spaces, the specific kind of warmth that wasn't for everyone but was fully real for the people who got to experience it that isn't something you find twice.

You deserve someone who knows how hard it is to find someone like you…
and chooses you every day like they're afraid to lose something that rare.
Not out of fear. Out of understanding.
Out of the full knowledge of what they have.

I hope you find that person, or I hope that person finds you. And I hope more than anything that when they do, you let yourself be chosen. That you don't underestimate what you bring, or talk yourself into settling, or convince yourself that ordinary is good enough when you are anything but.

You deserve someone who sees exactly how rare you are and never stops being grateful for it.

V — Surrender

The things I never said out loud.

There's a version of this where I said it sooner. Where I found the words before they became this complicated. Where I said the simple, terrifying thing instead of building walls of silence around it and calling that protection.

But I didn't. And I've made my peace with that mostly.

What I haven't made peace with is the idea that you might never know. That I might let this whole thing close without once being honest about what it was. And that felt worse than the vulnerability of saying it.

I won't say I love you —
because it only ever belonged to you anyway.
Those words didn't belong to anyone else.

But I will let you break my heart a thousand times.
Not because I'm weak.
But because some people are worth every single one.

There's a feeling in a song where someone says they'll come running the moment they're called. No conditions. No questions. Just presence, offered freely, because that's what it means to love someone without bargaining. That's what it means to show up not because you're owed something, but because that's simply who you are for that person.

I want you to know: I am that person for you. Whatever that means, wherever you are, whatever you need if you ever call, I will come. Not out of weakness. Not out of hope for something in return. Just because that's who I've been for you, quietly, all this time. And some things don't change just because they go unsaid.

"Main hamesha tumhara 'tu de diye awaaz re… main aa jaaunga' person rahunga."
VI — Acceptance

She may never accept my love. And that's okay.

I want to be honest about this part, because I think it's the hardest part and also, somehow, the most important.

I know you may not feel the same. I know this letter may not change anything. I know that love, even when it's real and quiet and full and patient, doesn't always find its way back to you. And I'm not writing this with the expectation that it will. I'm writing it because honesty, even when it changes nothing, has its own kind of value.

There's a painful clarity that comes with caring for someone who loves someone else the way you love them. I saw it once I watched you look at someone, and in that moment I understood what it felt like to be on the other side of something I'd only ever been on the inside of. It didn't make me love you less. It just made everything more honest. More clear. More real.

She may not accept my love. But my love never fades. It doesn't need to be accepted to be real. It doesn't need reciprocation to be true. It just is quietly, without asking permission.

And I've learned something about intentions. When they're pure when you care for someone without agenda, without calculation, without needing anything from them in return you don't lose people. People lose you. That's a truth I've had to sit with, and it's both painful and strangely freeing.

I choose to believe that love given honestly is never wasted. Even if it reaches no one. Even if it changes nothing. Even if it lives only in these words, in this moment, between you and me it counted. It mattered. It was real.

VI — The Real Mistake

Loving you was never the mistake. Thinking I could have you was.

I've spent a long time trying to figure out where things went wrong. Retracing steps. Asking myself whether I said too much or too little, whether I showed up too much or disappeared too quietly, whether there was a version of this where the outcome was different if I had just done something differently, somewhere, at some point.

And I've finally arrived at the answer.

Nothing went wrong with the love. The love was never the problem. Loving you was the most natural thing I have done, it required no decision, no convincing, no moment where I chose it. It simply happened, the way certain things happen, the way rain falls or seasons change. Without asking permission. Without caring whether it was convenient.

Loving you was honest. Loving you was real. Loving you was, if I'm being completely truthful, one of the purest things I've felt. And I refuse to call that a mistake. I refuse to look back on something that genuine and reduce it to an error in judgment.

The mistake if there was one was quieter than that. It was the moment I let the love become hope. The moment I crossed the line from feeling something to believing it might be returned. That's where I lost the ground beneath me.

Thinking I could have you. That's what undid me.

Not loving you that part was always safe, always clean, always mine to keep regardless of what you did or didn't feel. But somewhere along the way, the feeling grew an expectation. A quiet, half-formed belief that if I cared enough, if I was present enough, if the love was real enough it would somehow find its way to you and mean something. That you would feel it even without being told. That proximity and patience and sincerity would eventually add up to something.

They didn't. And that's not your fault. You didn't make me any promises. You didn't give me reasons to hope, I found those on my own, in the small moments I chose to read too much into, in the silences I filled with meaning that was never there. I built an entire architecture of possibility on a foundation you never laid.

That's the mistake. Not the love never the love. But the thinking. The hoping. The quiet, persistent belief that someone like you could ever be something I was allowed to have.

I know better now. Or I'm learning to.

The love stays. It always will. But the hope I'm finally, carefully, setting that down. Not because it hurt too much to carry. But because it was never mine to carry in the first place.

Loving you was never the mistake.

It never will be.

VII — No Regrets

I don't regret loving you this much. Not once. Not ever.

I know that might sound strange after everything I've just said. After admitting the pain of it, the silence of it, the weight of feeling unworthy, the ache of hoping for something that was never going to come. After all of that I want to be completely clear about something.

I don't regret a single moment of it.

Not the quiet caring. Not the poems that never landed. Not the feelings I carried longer than was comfortable, in places deeper than was reasonable. Not the mornings I thought about you before I'd even fully woken up. Not the moments I pulled back when I should have spoken, or the moments I noticed things about you that you'll never know I noticed.

None of it. Not one breath of it.

And I know I know that when I look back on my life, years from now, when enough time has passed to see things with the kind of clarity that distance brings, I won't look back at this and wince. I won't look back and think: why did I let myself feel that much? Why did I give that much to someone who didn't ask for it?

I'll look back and think: I loved someone completely. I loved someone the way they deserved to be loved, even if they never knew it. I didn't protect myself at the expense of the feeling. I let it be as big as it was.

And that, that I will never regret.

Regret requires believing you made the wrong choice. But I didn't choose this. It chose me. And the things that choose you the loves that arrive without permission and stay without invitation those aren't mistakes you can regret. They're just the truest parts of who you are.

I think some people spend their whole lives keeping themselves safe from feelings like this. Building careful walls, measuring how much they give, making sure they never love anyone more than they're loved back. And I understand that. I understand why the math of self-protection makes sense.

But I also think something is lost in that caution. Something important. The willingness to feel something fully even when it costs you, even when it doesn't come back, even when you end up writing a letter like this to someone who may never fully understand what they meant that willingness is not weakness. It's one of the bravest things a person can be.

I was brave enough to feel this. Completely. Without holding back.

And when I look back not now, not when it still has this much weight but years from now, when the edges have softened and the ache has settled into something quieter and more like gratitude I will be glad. Glad that I was capable of this. Glad that you were the person who brought it out of me. Glad that I didn't shrink from it or talk myself out of it or decide it wasn't worth the risk.

It was worth it. You were worth it.

Every single moment. Every single word. Every single feeling I never said out loud and every one I finally did.

No regrets, Clara. Not now. Not ever.

Not in this life.

VII — Unworthiness

Loving you while feeling like I wasn't enough.

This is the part I find hardest to write. Not because it isn't true but because admitting it makes it more real than I'd like it to be.

There's a specific kind of pain that doesn't get talked about enough. It isn't the pain of rejection, exactly. It's something quieter and more corrosive than that. It's the pain of loving someone and, in the same breath, believing you were never quite worthy of being loved back by them.

Not because they made you feel that way. That's the cruel part you didn't do anything to make me feel small. It was something I carried in on my own, long before you. A quiet voice that had been there for years, one that knows exactly when to speak up and it always chooses the moments when you're closest to something real, something that matters, someone who matters.

And it said: look at her. and look at you. do you really think you belong in the same sentence?

Loving someone and feeling unworthy of them is a different kind of pain. It doesn't announce itself. It just quietly shapes every decision you make every word you swallow, every moment you pull back, every time you almost said something and didn't.

I've thought about how much of my silence was caution, and how much of it was just this feeling like I didn't have the right to take up that kind of space in your life. Like my feelings, however real they were, were somehow presumptuous. Like wanting someone like you was already asking for more than I deserved.

And so I stayed small. I stayed quiet. I cared in all the ways that required nothing from you, because asking for anything felt like overreaching. I kept my feelings folded neatly inside me, like something too fragile to take out in public, too personal to risk.

Here's what I know now, looking back: that voice was lying. It always lies. It dresses itself up as humility, as realism, as not wanting to be embarrassing but what it really is, is fear. The fear that if you truly showed up, fully and honestly, you would be found lacking. That the person you care about would look at everything you are and decide it isn't quite enough.

Feeling unworthy of someone doesn't mean you are unworthy. It means you care so deeply that the stakes feel impossibly high. It means they matter to you in a way that terrifies you. It means the love is real so real that the possibility of it not being enough becomes the thing you carry everywhere, quietly, like a second heartbeat.

I carried that weight every time I was around you. And still even with all of it I wouldn't trade a single moment of having known you. Not one.

Because here's the thing about loving someone who makes you feel unworthy: they also, without trying, show you exactly the kind of person you want to become. The version of yourself that might one day feel like enough not for them, but for yourself. You were that mirror for me. And that is not a small thing.

VII — Not Enough

I just want to make you happy. But maybe I was never going to be enough for that.

This is the part of me I've protected the most. The part I've dressed up in poetry and metaphor and carefully chosen words because saying it plainly felt like too much exposure. Like standing somewhere with nothing to hide behind.

But here it is, plainly:

I just want you to be happy, Clara.

Not happy because of me. Not happy in the way I once quietly hoped where I was somehow part of the picture. Just happy. Genuinely, fully, in the way you deserve. The kind of happy that doesn't ask anything of you, that doesn't come with conditions or weight or the quiet expectation of something in return.

That's it. That's the whole of it. Underneath all the words, beneath every poem and every feeling I've tried to express, that's the thing that was always true: I wanted good things for you. More than I wanted anything for myself.

I think you are amazing, Clara. Not in the way people say it casually, as a compliment to fill silence. In the way that costs something to admit because saying it out loud means acknowledging how much you mattered, and how little I could do with that.

And I think, I have to be honest here, even though it's the hardest sentence in this entire letter I think I was never going to be enough for you.

Not because I didn't try. Not because I didn't feel it deeply enough or mean it sincerely enough. But because some people are built for each other in a way that has nothing to do with effort or intention or the size of what you feel. Because love, real love, isn't just about how much someone gives it's about whether what they give is what you actually need. And I'm not sure what I had was what you needed. I'm not sure I was the shape that fit.

Maybe you deserve better than me. I've thought this more times than I can count, and for a long time I told myself it was just insecurity speaking that quiet voice I mentioned earlier, the one that lies. But sometimes, when I'm being truly honest, I think it might just be true. Not as self-punishment. Not as a way of diminishing myself. Just as a clear-eyed understanding that certain people are meant for certain kinds of love, and the love I had to offer quiet, patient, unsaid may not have been the kind that could ever reach you.

You deserve someone whose love lands. Someone whose presence makes sense to you the way yours made sense to me. Someone you don't have to meet halfway, someone you run toward without thinking.

I don't know if I was ever that person. I suspect I wasn't. And I've had to make peace with the particular sadness of loving someone deeply and still understanding, somewhere underneath it all, that you might not have been what they needed either.

It doesn't make the love less real. It just makes it more honest.

I wanted to make you happy. I think you are the most amazing person I have known. And I think with the kind of love that wishes you well even at its own expense that you deserve every beautiful thing I wasn't able to give you.

I hope you find it. All of it.

VIII — Enough

Seeing her was enough. Even knowing she'd never be mine.

There is a kind of love that asks for nothing. Not because it has given up, not because it has stopped caring but because it has arrived at a place beyond wanting. A place where just existing near someone feels like more than you deserve, and somehow, more than enough.

I reached that place with you.

There were days when I'd see you just see you, across a room, going about your life completely unbothered by my presence and something in me would settle. Not ache. Not reach. Just… settle. Like a quiet exhale after holding your breath for too long. Like the world briefly making sense again without needing to explain why.

I knew, even then. I knew you were never going to be mine. Not in the way people mean when they say that word mine, with all its ownership and certainty and shared futures. I knew it the way you know certain things about yourself that you've never said out loud but have always understood. Quietly. Completely. Without drama.

And still seeing you was enough. Not almost enough. Not enough for now, until something better came along. Enough. Fully, completely, without condition.

I've tried to explain this to myself many times, because it doesn't make sense on the surface. How can seeing someone be enough when you feel so much? How can you look at a person you love and not feel the absence of what you'll never have?

But that's the thing it wasn't absence I felt when I saw you. It was presence. Yours. And that presence, even briefly, even from a distance, even in a moment that meant nothing to anyone else in the room it filled something. It reminded me that beauty exists. That people like you exist. That somewhere in the world, someone carries themselves with that kind of quiet grace, and I got to witness it.

There's a particular kind of peace that comes with loving someone without agenda. When you've let go of the outcome not because you forced yourself to, but because you genuinely arrived there the love becomes something pure. It stops being about you and what you want and starts being entirely about them. About hoping their days are good. About wanting them to be happy, even if that happiness has nothing to do with you. Especially then.

I wanted your happiness more than I wanted to be part of it. That is the truest thing I've ever felt.

So when I say seeing you was enough I mean it in the deepest sense. I mean that your existence in my world, however peripheral, however one-sided, was a gift I never asked for and never deserved and am endlessly grateful for.

Some people walk into your life and rearrange things quietly. They don't stay. They don't even know they were ever there. But the arrangement they leave behind the way you see people differently, the way you love more carefully, the way you understand yourself a little more honestly that stays forever.

You rearranged things for me. Quietly. Without knowing.

And seeing you just that, just you was always going to be enough.

I never needed you to be mine.

IX — Memory

How am I supposed to love someone else?

I've asked myself this question more honestly than I've ever admitted to anyone. How do you move forward when you're still carrying someone in the quietest parts of you? How do you make room for something new when there's a specific presence that hasn't quite left?

Not in a dramatic way. Not in the way that keeps you paralyzed or stops you from living. Just in the soft, persistent way that some people linger in a song that comes on unexpectedly, in a moment that reminds you of something they once said, in a dream where they appear without warning and feel more real than anything else for a few seconds after waking.

How am I supposed to love someone else… when it's still you I see in my dreams?

I'm not saying I won't. I'm not saying I can't. I'm just saying right now, in this honest space that you take up a specific kind of room that hasn't emptied yet. And I think that says something about the quality of a person, more than it says anything broken about me.

Some people don't forget you. They just carry you in the quietest ways. In the things they notice that remind them of you. In the standards they hold because of what they experienced with you. In the way they love differently more attentively, more patiently, more fully because they once loved someone who made them realize what love could actually look like.

You made me realize that. And for that alone, I am grateful.

X — Love & Fear

The difference between loving and being loved.

I've thought about this a lot the asymmetry of love. How it looks different from each side, how it creates different fears depending on which position you're in.

If someone truly loves you really, deeply, without performance their biggest fear is hurting you. Not losing you. Hurting you. Because when love is genuine, the other person's pain matters more than your own comfort. You'd rather carry the weight of an unspoken feeling for years than cause them a single moment of confusion or discomfort. You protect them, even from yourself.

But when someone is loved the way I loved you patiently, quietly, completely their biggest fear becomes something different. It becomes losing you. The fear isn't of pain. It's of absence. Of a world that no longer has your presence in it the way it once did.

Both fears are love. They just look different from the inside.

I've lived in both the fear of hurting you by saying too much, and the fear of losing you by saying nothing. I chose silence for a long time because the first fear was louder. But I think, in the end, the second one deserved to be heard too.

So here it is. Finally heard.

XI — Admiration

What I see when I look at you.

I want to end with this not because it's the most profound thing I've said, but because it's the most human. The simplest. The most honest.

You have no idea.

You have no idea how you look through my eyes. Not just physically though that too, without apology but in every way. The way you hold yourself. The way your energy fills a space without trying to. The way you are exactly who you are without performing it, without adjusting it for anyone. There's a specific beauty in a person who is simply, fully themselves and you have that in a way that's hard to look away from.

"Tumhe koi idea bhi nahi hai… tum meri nazron mein kitni khoobsurat ho."

You are beautiful in the way that matters not just the kind you can see, but the kind you feel. The kind that stays with you. The kind that, months later, you still find yourself thinking about.

I hope someone tells you this often. I hope someone says it not as a line, not as a strategy, but as a genuine, simple truth the way I mean it right now. I hope the people in your life see you the way I see you and make sure you know it.

Because you deserve to know it. You deserve to feel it. Every single day.

XII — The Lucky One

How lucky the guy who gets her without even praying for it.

I say this with a smile. A real one the kind that sits somewhere between warmth and ache, between genuinely meaning it and genuinely wishing things were different.

Because somewhere out there is a person who will get you. Not earn you, not chase you, not write thousands of words trying to find a way to tell you what you mean to them. Just get you. Effortlessly. No idea that there are people in this world who would have given everything every quiet prayer, every honest word, every piece of themselves just to be in the position he'll occupy without a second thought.

I hope he sees you. Really sees you not just the surface of you, not just the easy parts, but all of it. The rare parts. The parts I saw from a distance and spent this entire letter trying to put into words. I hope he wakes up every day and understands, at least a little, what he has.

XII — Still Not Enough

I wrote poetry. And it still wasn't enough.

I want to tell you something I've been sitting with for a long time something that feels important to say, especially here, at the end of all these words.

I wrote poetry for you. Not in the way people write poetry to impress someone, not as performance, not as strategy. I wrote it because there was something living inside me that had no other shape. Something that kept pressing against the walls of ordinary language and finding it too small, too flat, too insufficient.

So I reached for metaphors. For images. For the kind of language that bends toward feeling instead of just describing it. I wrote about silence and storms and the way certain people change the quality of a room just by being in it. I wrote about choosing and losing and carrying and the difference between wanting and keeping. I wrote more words about you than I have written about anything in my life.

And when I finished every single time I read it back and thought: this still isn't it. This still doesn't touch it.

There is something about you, Clara, that language keeps failing to reach. I don't say that as a compliment dressed up in pretty words. I say it as a confession the most honest one in this entire letter.

I am sure you understood my intentions. I think you always did, even when I said nothing, even when I was careful and quiet and gave nothing away. Some people just know. And I think you are one of those people perceptive in the way that makes you impossible to hide things from, even when you're not trying to find them.

But understanding someone's intentions and being moved by them are two completely different things. And I think that's what I have to finally make peace with. You understood. And it didn't change anything. Not because you are cold, not because you are unkind but because understanding is not the same as feeling. Because knowing someone loves you is not the same as loving them back. Because the heart doesn't respond to effort or intention or even the most carefully chosen words.

It responds to what it responds to. And that is nobody's fault.

What I find hardest to hold the thing that sits in my chest like something unresolved is that I gave this everything I had. Every honest word, every quiet moment of care, every poem that tried and failed to say the unsayable. I emptied myself out trying to find the expression that would finally be enough. And none of it was. Not because it wasn't real. But because some feelings are simply larger than the vessels we try to pour them into.

Language has limits. Poetry has limits. Even this letter all of it, every word has limits. And you sit somewhere beyond all of them, unchanged, as you always were, as you always will be in the way I carry you.

I wrote everything I could write. I felt everything I could feel. I expressed what most people never find the courage to express at all.

And it still wasn't enough to move you.

I think I will spend a long time learning to be okay with that.

I wrote you into metaphors,
into late-night lines that never quite landed,
into every honest thing I've ever put to paper.
 
And you remained as you always were
larger than the words,
quieter than the feeling,
and entirely out of reach.

to keep trying to say it

XIII — 1 in 7 Billion

To me, she was 1 in 7 billion. To her, I was just 1 in 7 billion.

I don't think there's a more honest way to say it than that.

Not cruel. Not dramatic. Just true in that quiet, devastating way that certain truths arrive. The kind that don't shout. The kind that sit down beside you and wait for you to finally look at them.

To me, you were singular. One of one. The kind of person that only makes sense as a once-in-a-lifetime encounter not because I hadn't met remarkable people before, but because something about you was different in a way I couldn't rationalize or reduce. You weren't remarkable because you were perfect. You were remarkable because you were irreplaceable. Because I could not have found you again if I tried. Because the specific combination of everything you are the way you carried yourself, the way you made a room feel, the way you existed so fully without performing any of it that wasn't something the world was manufacturing in any quantity.

You were 1 in 7 billion. I meant that with every part of me.

And to you I was just someone. One of the many. A face in the crowd of people who pass through a life without leaving a mark deep enough to notice. Not because I was ordinary. But because the heart only has room to make certain people extraordinary, and I was never the one you made that room for.

That's not a complaint. I want to be clear about that. I'm not writing this to make you feel guilty for something you never chose. You can't decide who becomes irreplaceable to you any more than I could decide that it would be you. These things happen to us not because of us.

But I think it's the most accurate way to describe what this was. The entire asymmetry of it. The whole painful, beautiful, lopsided truth of loving someone who doesn't love you back not because they are heartless, but because they simply never saw you the way you saw them. Because you were looking at a universe and they were looking at a person. And those are two completely different kinds of looking.

I looked at you and saw everything. You looked at me and saw someone.

There are 7 billion people on this earth. And somehow, impossibly, the one I found most irreplaceable was the one for whom I was most replaceable. That's not a tragedy. It's just the mathematics of love unequal, unsolvable, and entirely indifferent to how much you feel.

I have thought about what it would take to be someone's 1 in 7 billion. Whether it's something you earn or something you simply are to the right person. Whether I am that for someone else someone I haven't met yet, someone who would look at me the way I looked at you and feel the same impossible weight of it.

I hope I am. I hope everyone gets to be that for someone.

But I also know that you were that for me and that no matter what comes next, no matter who else walks into my life, that specific thing will never be repeated. You don't find your 1 in 7 billion twice.

You just carry them. Quietly. For as long as it takes.

To me 1 in 7 billion.
The only one the world could have made
that would have undone me this quietly.
 
To her 1 in 7 billion.
Just another number.
Just another someone
in a world full of someones.
 
Same words.
Completely different weight.
That's love, I think
the most unequal equation
no one warned you about.
